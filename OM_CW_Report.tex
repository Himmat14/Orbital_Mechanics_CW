\section*{Part (a): One-Year Orbit Propagation}
%──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection*{(a.i) Numerical Method}

The perturbed equations of motion in the ECI frame are (see \cite[L7, eq.~77]{notes}):
\begin{equation}
    \ddot{\bm{r}} + \frac{\mu}{r^3}\bm{r} = \bm{a}_d,
    \label{eq:eom}
\end{equation}
where $\bm{a}_d = \bm{a}_{J2} + \bm{a}_{\mathrm{drag}} + \bm{a}_{\mathrm{SRP}} + \bm{a}_{\mathrm{lsol}}$. This is integrated directly (Cowell's method \cite[L7]{notes}) using the \textbf{DOP853} 8th-order Dormand--Prince explicit Runge--Kutta scheme via \texttt{scipy.integrate.solve\_ivp}, with tolerances $\varepsilon_r = 10^{-10}$, $\varepsilon_a = 10^{-12}$, and maximum step size \SI{300}{s} to resolve perigee passage. Output is sampled every \SI{1}{h}; Keplerian elements are recovered from each state using the standard inversion \cite[L5]{notes}.

\subsection*{(a.ii) Perturbation Models}

\paragraph{J2 -- Earth oblateness.}
From the spherical harmonics expansion of the geopotential \cite[L8, eq.~106--107]{notes}, truncated at degree 2:
\begin{equation}
    \bm{a}_{J2} = \frac{3}{2}\frac{\mu J_2 R_E^2}{r^5}
    \begin{pmatrix}
        x\!\left(5z^2/r^2 - 1\right) \\
        y\!\left(5z^2/r^2 - 1\right) \\
        z\!\left(5z^2/r^2 - 3\right)
    \end{pmatrix},
    \qquad J_2 = 1.0826\times10^{-3}.
    \label{eq:j2}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Atmospheric drag.}
The drag acceleration acting opposite to the velocity relative to the co-rotating atmosphere is:
\begin{equation}
    \bm{a}_{\mathrm{drag}} = -\tfrac{1}{2}C_D\frac{A}{m}\rho\,v_{\mathrm{rel}}^2\,\hat{\bm{v}}_{\mathrm{rel}},
    \qquad C_D=2.2,\; \frac{A}{m}=\frac{20}{700}\;\text{m}^2\,\text{kg}^{-1}.
    \label{eq:drag}
\end{equation}
The density $\rho$ is from a layered exponential atmosphere. At the AURORA perigee altitude of \SI{1602}{km}, $\rho\approx 1.9\times10^{-16}$\,kg\,m$^{-3}$, giving $|\bm{a}_{\mathrm{drag}}|\approx 5.4\times10^{-10}$\,m\,s$^{-2}$.

\paragraph{Solar radiation pressure (SRP).}
\begin{equation}
    \bm{a}_{\mathrm{SRP}} = \nu C_R \frac{A}{m} P_\odot \left(\frac{\mathrm{AU}}{r_\odot}\right)^{\!2} \hat{\bm{r}}_{\odot},
    \qquad P_\odot = \SI{4.56e-6}{N\,m^{-2}},\; C_R=1.5,
    \label{eq:srp}
\end{equation}
where $\nu\in\{0,1\}$ is the cylindrical eclipse indicator. SRP is approximately constant over the orbit at $|\bm{a}_{\mathrm{SRP}}|\approx1.95\times10^{-7}$\,m\,s$^{-2}$.

\paragraph{Lunisolar third-body.}
The indirect-term formulation for Sun ($\odot$) and Moon ($\mathcal{L}$) is \cite[L8]{notes}:
\begin{equation}
    \bm{a}_{\mathrm{lsol}} = \mu_\odot\!\left(\frac{\bm{r}_\odot-\bm{r}}{|\bm{r}_\odot-\bm{r}|^3} - \frac{\bm{r}_\odot}{r_\odot^3}\right)
    + \mu_\mathcal{L}\!\left(\frac{\bm{r}_\mathcal{L}-\bm{r}}{|\bm{r}_\mathcal{L}-\bm{r}|^3} - \frac{\bm{r}_\mathcal{L}}{r_\mathcal{L}^3}\right).
    \label{eq:lsol}
\end{equation}
At apogee ($r_a\approx\SI{45220}{km}$), $|\bm{a}_{\mathrm{lsol}}|\approx8.1\times10^{-6}$\,m\,s$^{-2}$ -- the second-largest perturbation. \cref{fig:perturb} shows all four magnitudes over one orbital period.

The Gauss variational equations \cite[L7, eq.~96--102]{notes} identify which components of $\bm{a}_d$ drive each element. Averaging over one orbit \cite[L8, eq.~109]{notes} isolates secular trends:

\paragraph{Semi-major axis $a$.}
From the Gauss equation \cite[L7, eq.~102]{notes},
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\mathrm{d}a}{\mathrm{d}t} = \frac{2a^2}{h}\!\left(e\sin\theta\,a_{d,r} + \frac{h^2}{\mu r}\,a_{d,s}\right).
\end{equation}
The J2 average is zero (conservative); drag reduces $a$ but at perigee altitude \SI{1602}{km} the effect is negligible ($|\bm{a}_{\mathrm{drag}}|\approx5\times10^{-10}$\,m\,s$^{-2}$). Simulation gives $\Delta a = +\SI{0.77}{km}$ over one year, with short-period oscillations of $\sim\SI{10}{km}$ amplitude driven by J2.

\paragraph{Eccentricity $e$.}
The averaged J2 rate gives $\dot{\bar{e}}=0$ \cite[L8]{notes}. The observed $\Delta e\approx+0.008$ arises from lunisolar long-period coupling (Kozai--Lidov type), with $\sim$90-day period oscillations visible in \cref{fig:orb_elems}.

\paragraph{Inclination $i$.}
Averaged J2 gives $\dot{\bar{i}}=0$ \cite[L8]{notes}. The residual $\Delta i\approx -0.18°$ over one year reflects lunar/solar out-of-plane perturbations at the same 90-day timescale as $e$.

\paragraph{RAAN $\Omega$.}
Averaging the J2 term in the Gauss equation \cite[L7, eq.~99]{notes} yields the secular precession \cite[L8]{notes}:
\begin{equation}
    \dot{\bar{\Omega}} = -\frac{3}{2}n J_2\!\left(\frac{R_E}{p}\right)^{\!2}\cos i,
    \qquad p = a(1-e^2),\quad n=\sqrt{\mu/a^3}.
    \label{eq:raan}
\end{equation}
With $i=116.6°$, $\cos i = -0.449 < 0$, so $\dot{\bar{\Omega}}>0$ (retrograde orbit drifts eastward). The analytical estimate gives $\dot{\bar{\Omega}}\approx+0.126°$\,day$^{-1}=+45.9°$\,yr$^{-1}$, in excellent agreement with the simulated $\Delta\Omega=+45.9°$.

\paragraph{Argument of perigee $\omega$.}
From the averaged J2 equations \cite[L8]{notes}:
\begin{equation}
    \dot{\bar{\omega}} = \frac{3}{4}n J_2\!\left(\frac{R_E}{p}\right)^{\!2}\!\left(5\cos^2 i - 1\right).
    \label{eq:omega}
\end{equation}
The AURORA inclination $i=116.6°=180°-63.4°$ satisfies the \emph{retrograde critical inclination} condition \cite[L8]{notes}:
\begin{equation}
    5\cos^2 i_c - 1 = 0 \implies i_c = 180° - 63.435°,
\end{equation}
so $\dot{\bar{\omega}}=0$ secularly. The orbit is thus a \textbf{frozen orbit}, maintaining a fixed apogee location in inertial space. The residual drift $\Delta\omega=+0.34°$\,yr$^{-1}$ is due to higher-order J2 and lunisolar terms.

\section*{Part (b): Ground Station Pass Analysis}
%──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

For ground-station visibility the ECI state is rotated to ECEF via \cite[L5, eq.~48]{notes}:
\begin{equation}
    \bm{r}_{\mathrm{ECEF}} = \mathbf{R}_3[\theta_{\mathrm{GMST}}]\,\bm{r}_{\mathrm{ECI}},
    \qquad \theta_{\mathrm{GMST}} = \theta_{\mathrm{GMST},0} + \omega_\oplus t,
\end{equation}
and transformed to the ENZ topocentric frame. The elevation angle $\varepsilon$ is:
\begin{equation}
    \varepsilon = \arcsin\!\left(\frac{\bm{\rho}\cdot\hat{\bm{Z}}}{|\bm{\rho}|}\right), \qquad \bm{\rho} = \bm{r}_{\mathrm{ECEF}} - \bm{r}_{\mathrm{GS}},
\end{equation}
where $\hat{\bm{Z}}$ is the local zenith unit vector. A pass is defined by $\varepsilon\geq5°$. The trajectory is integrated for 72 hours at \SI{30}{s} steps (DOP853, same tolerances as Part a), and results are filtered to the 48-hour window 1--3 Jan 2026 12:00 UTC.

\subsection*{(b.ii) Comparison of Pass Durations}

The contact-time ratio is $2028/39 \approx 52$. This is explained geometrically as follows.

The AURORA orbit has $a=\SI{26600}{km}$, $e=0.7$, giving $r_p = a(1-e)=\SI{7980}{km}$ and $r_a = a(1+e)=\SI{45220}{km}$ \cite[L2]{notes}. By Kepler's second law ($h=r^2\dot\theta=\mathrm{const}$\cite[L1]{notes}), the spacecraft spends most of its 12-hour period near apogee. The Earth horizon angular radius at apogee is:
\begin{equation}
    \rho_h = \arccos\!\left(\frac{R_E}{r_a}\right) = \arccos\!\left(\frac{6378}{45220}\right) \approx 81.8°.
\end{equation}

The orbital inclination $i=116.6°$ is retrograde, so the maximum sub-satellite latitude is $|180°-116.6°|=63.4°$ \cite[L5]{notes}. Malarg\"{u}e ($\phi=-35°$) lies well inside this envelope and is frequently overflown; when apogee passes over South America a single pass lasts 3--7 hours. The orbit begins at $\nu=180°$ (apogee above South America) so Pass 1 starts at epoch.

Kiruna ($\phi=67°$) lies \emph{outside} the ground track envelope ($63.4°\mathrm{N}$). It is only visible when apogee altitude places it within the $\rho_h=81.8°$ horizon cone -- occurring approximately once per sidereal day when the ascending node aligns with northern Europe. Despite this, the nearly overhead geometry at apogee yields $\varepsilon_{\max}\approx72°$, counter-intuitively exceeding many Malarg\"{u}e passes ($\sim57°$), because the spacecraft altitude is so large that the apparent angular distance to Kiruna from the sub-satellite point is only $\sim4°$.

Over 48 hours the two trajectories are indistinguishable: pass count is identical and total contact time differs by $<\SI{1}{min}$. The dominant perturbation driving trajectory divergence is the J2 RAAN precession (eq.~\ref{eq:raan}). Over 2 days:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta\Omega_{48\mathrm{h}} = \dot{\bar{\Omega}}\times 2 = 0.126°\,\mathrm{day}^{-1}\times 2 = 0.25°,
\end{equation}
a shift too small to affect pass schedules. Over one year, however, the accumulated $\Delta\Omega=+45.9°$ shifts the ground track by nearly $\tfrac{1}{8}$ orbit, substantially altering coverage geometry for both stations. Keplerian prediction is therefore adequate for short-arc ($<$1 week) scheduling, but high-fidelity propagation (eq.~\ref{eq:eom}) is required for operational mission planning.
